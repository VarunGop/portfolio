Projects

Double Sequential External Defibrillation (DSED) for Pre-Hospital Refractory Ventricular Fibrillation

Role: Project Lead
Project Duration: August 2024 - Present

DSED has emerged as a powerful defibrillation technique for the treatment of ventricular fibrillation refractory to conventional defibrillation strategies. DSED, however, currently requires two defibrillators to perform, making it impractical, if not impossible, for EMS to perform in the field.

My team of medical students and myself are currently working with physicians, EMS, to design and test a device to allow EMS providers to easily perform DSED without needing a secondary defibrillator, increasing their ability to perform DSED and treat refractory ventricular fibrillation.

Adjustable Orbital Rim Implant Surgical Guide

Role: Project Lead and Designer
Project Duration: July 2024 - Present

Orbital floor fractures are a common facial fracture affecting the inferior orbit, or eye socket. These fractures can cause signficant changes for patients by altering the natural set point of their eyes in their sockets and affecting vision and balance. Current treatment is the placement of a titanium implant under the eye on top of the fracture.

Unfortunately, oftentimes these fractures cannot be visualized mid-operation forcing providers to place these implants using only their clinical judgement. I am leading a team of medical students and Oral and Maxiliofacial Surgeons to develop an adjustable surgical guide to assist surgeons in the placement of these implants to reduce complications and improve patient outcomes.

Biomedical Recognizer of Anatomy and Endotracheal Tube Holder (BRAETH)

Role: Project Lead and Lead Designer
Project Duration: September 2023 - Present

BRAETH was created by a six person team with fellow UMass Amherst biomedical engineering students. Under the supervision of Baystate Medical Center trauma surgeon, Dr. Tovy Kamine, the team and I developed a low-cost 3D printed device to assist providers with the performance of cricothyrotomies, a life-saving procedure for patients who are unable to breathe through their mouth or nose.

BRAETH performed extremely well in preliminary testing, reducing the time for providers to quickly perform a cricothyrotomy, and increasing provider confidence with the procedure. The team is currently in the process of filing a provisional patent for BRAETH, and are currently testing the device in larger studies, with the aim of filing for a full patent in the near future. Findings are being presented at ACS: A Dialogue on Surgical Simulation in March of 2025.

ProjectEHR

Role: Front-End Developer
Project Duration: June 2024 - Present

I am currently working on a project with fellow medical students to develop a fully web based electronic health record system that can be used by medical students in their early training to increase their familiarity with EHR systems. I am mainly focused on the frontend development, making the UI as similar to current EHR systems as possible. I am additionally helping with the creation and conduction of studies to test the impact of early EHR training on medical student confidence.

Customized Machine Learning Facial Emotion Recognition Algorithm

Role: Individual project
Project Duration: September 2023 - May 2024

For my honors thesis project, I individually developed a machine learning algorithm trained on custom pictures of a singular subject to recognize human emotions from facial expressions. I tested this model against one trained on a generic set of data, like those currently used in facial emotion detection algorithms.

When tested on subjects, the custom model was able to perform on par with the generic model in the detection of happiness and sadness, but significantly outperformed the generic model in detecion of anger. This project reveals a potential mechanism for the emotional state monitoring of patients, especially those who express emotions in unconventional ways, allowing medical providers a noninvasive way to monitor everyone's emotions. I presented a poster of this work at the Massachusetts Undergraduate Research Conference.
